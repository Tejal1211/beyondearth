SECTION 01 / NAVIGATION

Our Solar Neighborhood

Eight worlds, one shared orbit around a single star. Each carries a different chapter of where we're headed next.

☉

Mercury

88-day orbit · 167°C swings

Closest to the sun, and first on the list for solar-collector outposts that need nothing but raw light.

♀

Venus

462°C surface · crushing pressure

Too hostile below the clouds — but 50km up, the air pressure matches Earth's. Floating cities are on the table.

⊕

Earth

Home · 4.5 billion years running

The only data point we have for a civilization that works. Every plan beyond here starts from what we learned here.

♂

Mars

225 million km · 24h 37m day

The nearest world we could actually live on. The next section is entirely about what that looks like.

♃

Jupiter

79+ moons · no solid surface

Not a destination — a gravity well we use to slingshot probes further out for almost no fuel.

♄

Saturn

7 ring groups · Titan's thick haze

Titan's moon has lakes of liquid methane and an atmosphere thick enough to fly through on muscle power alone.

SECTION 02 / SETTLEMENT

A City on Mars, 2075

Pressurized domes, vertical farms, and a grid that never sees blackout. Here's what the first real Martian city has to solve.

⟁

Transportation

Maglev tunnels link domes underground; electric VTOL skiffs handle surface transit above the dust line.

⚡

Energy

Compact fusion cores backed by sub-surface geothermal taps — built to run straight through dust-storm season.

⌂

Population

50,000 residents across nine linked domes, with life support engineered for a 2% annual margin of growth.

◈

AI Systems

An autonomous grid manages air, water, and crop cycles — flagging failures hours before a human would notice.

▣

Architecture

Regolith-printed shells shield against radiation; every dome is grown in layers by robotic printer-arms.

SECTION 04 / CAPABILITY

The Tools That Get Us There

None of the above works without these five technologies clearing their final hurdles.

Artificial Intelligence

Mission systems that plan, predict failure, and re-route resources faster than any ground crew could.

Quantum Computing

Trajectory and materials problems that took supercomputer-months collapse to overnight runs.

Fusion Energy

Net-positive reactors small enough to ship, powerful enough to run a colony through total darkness.

Space Elevators

A carbon-nanotube tether to orbit, cutting launch costs from a fortune to a freight fare.

Robotics

Construction crews that don't need air, sleep, or rescue when the work gets dangerous.
